## Introduction

This page explores the theoretical foundations of IntentSpec and its impact on autonomous agent safety. As LLM-driven agents begin to manage private keys and execute onchain transactions, the gap between syntactic correctness and semantic intent becomes a critical attack surface.

## 1. The Entropy of Automation

The Ethereum Virtual Machine (EVM) enforces **syntactic correctness** through type checking, gas metering, and revert conditions. However, it does not enforce **semantic intent**.

A transaction that is syntactically valid may:

- Transfer funds to an unintended recipient
- Execute a function with unintended economic consequences
- Violate the user's actual goals while appearing programmatically correct

This creates what we call the **semantic gap**: the distance between what a function *can* do (as described by its ABI) and what it *should* do (as intended by the user).

<Warning>
As LLM-driven agents begin to manage private keys, the semantic gap becomes a critical vulnerability. Agents operating with incomplete information may execute syntactically valid but semantically catastrophic transactions.
</Warning>

## 2. The Formal Problem

Without structured metadata, agents perform an estimation:

$$
T(\text{ABI}, \text{Bytecode}) \rightarrow \text{Intent}_{\text{Estimated}}
$$

Where:

- **ABI** provides function signatures and parameter types
- **Bytecode** is the compiled contract logic
- **T** is an inference function (typically LLM-based pattern matching)
- **Intent_Estimated** is the agent's best guess at what the function does

### The Variance Problem

Because the ABI is sparse and bytecode is opaque, the variance of $T$ is high:

$$
\text{Var}(\text{Intent}_{\text{Estimated}}) \propto \frac{1}{\text{Information}(\text{ABI})}
$$

Less information in the ABI leads to higher uncertainty in intent estimation. This uncertainty compounds over multiple transactions, leading to **agentic drift**: the gradual divergence between the agent's understanding and the contract's actual behavior.

### Agentic Drift

Let $I_t$ be the agent's intent estimate at transaction $t$. With each transaction, the estimate accumulates error:

$$
I_t = I_{t-1} + \epsilon_t
$$

Where $\epsilon_t$ is the estimation error at time $t$. Over $n$ transactions:

$$
\text{Total Error} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \epsilon_i
$$

Without ground-truth correction, this error grows unbounded.

## 3. The Verification Loop

IntentSpec introduces a structured **verification loop** that replaces estimation with validation:

<Steps>
  <Step title="Discover">
    Agent retrieves structured metadata $M$ from `getIntentSpecURI()`:
    
    $$
    M = \{ \text{intent}, \text{preconditions}, \text{effects}, \text{risks} \}
    $$
  </Step>
  
  <Step title="Validate Intent">
    Agent checks that user goal $G$ aligns with declared intent:
    
    $$
    \text{similarity}(G, M.\text{intent}) > \theta
    $$
    
    Where $\theta$ is a confidence threshold.
  </Step>
  
  <Step title="Check Preconditions">
    Agent verifies all preconditions $P_i \in M.\text{preconditions}$ are satisfied:
    
    $$
    \forall P_i : \text{verify}(P_i, \text{State}) = \text{true}
    $$
  </Step>
  
  <Step title="Simulate">
    Agent simulates transaction and observes state changes $S$:
    
    $$
    S = \text{simulate}(\text{Transaction}, \text{State})
    $$
  </Step>
  
  <Step title="Verify Effects">
    Agent confirms simulated state changes match declared effects:
    
    $$
    \forall E_i \in M.\text{effects} : \text{match}(E_i, S) = \text{true}
    $$
  </Step>
</Steps>

This loop provides **constraint-based interaction**: the agent operates within declared boundaries rather than guessing from sparse signals.

## 4. Impact on Safety

With structured metadata as a semantic anchor, the probability of intent failure changes significantly.

Let $P(f)$ be the probability of a "failure of intent" (agent executes a transaction that does not match user goals).

With a semantic anchor $A$ representing the quality and completeness of metadata:

$$
P(f)_{\text{ASCD}} = P(f) \cdot e^{-k \cdot A}
$$

Where:

- **ASCD** = Agent-Safe Contract Documentation (contracts implementing IntentSpec)
- $k$ is a scaling constant representing agent verification capabilities
- $A \in [0, 1]$ is the metadata quality score

### Interpretation

As metadata quality $A$ increases (more complete preconditions, effects, and risk disclosures), the failure probability decreases exponentially. This suggests that even partial adoption of IntentSpec can yield significant safety improvements.

### Metadata Quality Score

We define $A$ as:

$$
A = w_1 \cdot \text{Completeness} + w_2 \cdot \text{Precision} + w_3 \cdot \text{Verifiability}
$$

Where:

- **Completeness**: Fraction of functions with declared intent
- **Precision**: Specificity of preconditions and effects
- **Verifiability**: Ability to programmatically check claims
- $w_i$ are weights summing to 1

## 7. Systemic Risk Reduction

Beyond individual transaction safety, IntentSpec addresses **systemic risk** in agentic economies.

### Flash Crashes and Cascade Failures

In DeFi, agents operating with incomplete information can trigger cascade failures:

1. Agent A misinterprets a liquidation function
2. Agent A executes unintended liquidations
3. Market prices shift unexpectedly
4. Agents B, C, D react to price signals
5. Positive feedback loop creates "flash crash"

With IntentSpec:

- Agents validate intent before execution
- Risk disclosures prompt additional checks
- Simulation catches unexpected state changes
- Cascade is interrupted before execution

### Game-Theoretic Considerations

IntentSpec changes the incentive structure for contract developers:

- **Without metadata**: Agents avoid complex contracts (too risky) → reduces protocol adoption
- **With metadata**: Agents can safely interact with complex contracts → increases "agentic liquidity"

This creates a **positive-sum equilibrium** where:

- Developers gain agent users by implementing IntentSpec
- Agents gain access to more protocols by supporting IntentSpec
- Users benefit from safer, more capable autonomous systems

## 8. Limitations and Open Problems

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Metadata Misrepresentation">
    Malicious developers can provide false metadata. Agents must independently verify:
    
    - Bytecode analysis to detect obvious mismatches
    - Simulation to compare declared vs. actual effects
    - Reputation systems to track metadata accuracy over time
    
    **Open problem**: Automated verification of semantic claims against bytecode behavior.
  </Accordion>
  
  <Accordion title="Incomplete Specifications">
    Not all state changes can be easily described in natural language. Complex interactions may require formal specifications.
    
    **Future work**: Integration with formal verification tools (e.g., Certora, Halmos) to provide machine-checkable proofs alongside natural language metadata.
  </Accordion>
  
  <Accordion title="Agent Capability Variance">
    Different agents have different capabilities for parsing and verifying metadata. A less sophisticated agent may misinterpret complex preconditions.
    
    **Mitigation**: Standardized testing suites for agent metadata interpretation.
  </Accordion>
  
  <Accordion title="Evolution Over Time">
    Contracts may be upgraded via proxies. Metadata must evolve with contract logic.
    
    **Best practice**: Update metadata URI on upgrade and maintain versioned history.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## 9. Conclusion

The semantic safety frontier represents a fundamental shift in how we think about agent-contract interaction.

By providing structured, machine-verifiable metadata, IntentSpec:

1. Reduces estimation variance
2. Enables constraint-based reasoning
3. Provides explicit risk boundaries
4. Supports simulation-based validation
5. Reduces systemic cascade risk

As autonomous agents become more prevalent in onchain systems, the semantic gap between syntactic correctness and semantic intent becomes the dominant attack surface.

<Note>
IntentSpec is not a complete solution to agent safety. It is a necessary primitive that enables safer agent-contract interaction. Additional layers (reputation, formal verification, economic incentives) are required for a comprehensive safety framework.
</Note>

The future of agentic economies depends on our ability to bridge the semantic gap. IntentSpec is a step toward that future.
